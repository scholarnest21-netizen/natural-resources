2.1 Classification of Natural Resources

How resources are categorized based on origin and replenishability

📖 What are Natural Resources?

Natural resources are the raw materials provided by nature that are essential for life on Earth. They include substances from the living and non-living world that humans transform into valuable goods and services. Examples include soil, timber, oil, water, minerals, and even scenic landscapes.

♻️ Renewable Resources

Resources that can be continuously harvested with proper planning. They replenish through natural ecological cycles.

Solar EnergyWind Energy ForestsWater AnimalsPlants
Key Point: Sustainable use means consuming at a rate ≤ the rate of replacement.

⛽ Non-Renewable Resources

Resources that cannot be replenished by natural means. Once used up, they have very little chance of recovery. They must be used wisely for future generations.

CoalPetroleum MineralsNatural Gas
Warning: Being consumed at an alarmingly high rate. Exhaustible resources!

⚠️ Problems Associated with Natural Resources

  • Unequal Consumption: Developed nations consume far more — the USA with just 4% of world population uses ~25% of world resources.
  • Animal food requires more land: Countries dependent on non-vegetarian diets need larger areas for pastureland.
  • Corruption: Natural resource sectors are fertile ground for corruption across developing nations.
  • Environmental impact: Every stage of extraction–processing–manufacturing–disposal affects the environment.
  • Water scarcity: Lack of potable water leads to disease epidemics like cholera and HIV-related illness.

2.2 Forest Resources

Types, uses, products, and the crisis of deforestation

🌳 What is a Forest?

A forest is a biotic community with a predominance of trees. It is one of the most precious gifts of nature — restoring oxygen, providing medicines, regulating climate, controlling water run-off, and providing food and shelter for wildlife. India has 16 major forest types subdivided into 221 minor types.

✅ Direct Uses of Forests

  • Timber — plywood, doors, windows, furniture, paper
  • Raw materials — for large & small-scale industries (bamboo, beedi, seed oil)
  • Edible products — fruits, nuts, seeds, spices, fodder for animals
  • Natural habitat — for tribal people (food, fodder, medicines)
  • Manure & fuel wood — critical energy for rural population
  • National income — recreation, hunting, fishing; projected ₹2 billion/annum by 2020
  • Medicinal plants — hundreds of compounds of medicinal value
  • Tourism & ecotourism — wildlife tourism growing rapidly in India
  • Employment — forest sector contributes to GDP

🔄 Indirect Uses of Forests

  • Minimize natural hazards (floods, droughts, landslides)
  • Retain water in soil → prevent drought
  • Reduce soil erosion & siltation of water bodies
  • Reduce desertification; add humus to soil
  • Provide habitat for wildlife; maintain biodiversity
  • Regulate hydrological cycle through transpiration
  • Regulate atmospheric gases — absorb CO₂, release O₂
  • An average tree inhales 12 kg of CO₂ per year, enough O₂ for a family of four

💥 Effects of Deforestation

  • Loss of natural habitat → loss of biodiversity
  • Increased frequency & intensity of floods
  • Land degradation & soil erosion
  • Loss of forest products (fuel, food, medicine)
  • Climate change — deforestation causes ~10% of greenhouse gases
  • Siltation of rivers and lakes
  • Reduced rainfall & increased drought
  • Uprooting of indigenous tribal people
📍 Case Study

Deforestation in Andaman & Nicobar Islands

The British established a Forest Department in 1883, and large-scale timber extraction using convict labour led to massive deforestation. This caused severe soil erosion, heavy sedimentation in coastal waters, and death of coral reefs. Species like the saltwater crocodile and Andaman wild pig are now endangered.

🏔️ Case Study

World's Wettest Area Dries Up — Cherrapunji

Once the wettest spot in India, Cherrapunji's mixed natural forests in the catchment area have been destroyed. With no forests to hold water, most of the 12,000 mm annual rainfall quickly runs off. Springs and rivers dry up after monsoon, causing acute drinking water shortages. Water is now supplied by trucks!

🛡️ Control of Deforestation

  • Mining should be prohibited in protected forest areas
  • Tree felling should be followed by massive plantation drives
  • Environmental laws must be strictly enforced
  • Forest extension through social forestry, agro forestry, recreation forestry
  • Public awareness about medicinal and environmental significance of forests
🌱 Environmental Movement — Chipko: Under Sunderlal Bahuguna's leadership in 1995, women in Tehri Garhwal tied a sacred thread around 1,000 trees and hugged trees marked for felling to prevent cutting. The movement spread 4,870 km from Kashmir to Kohima!

2.3 Water Resources

Water cycle, sources, calamities, conflicts, and dams

💧 Importance of Water

Water is the elixir of life — an essential component of all living things. It determines weather, shapes land, and regulates climate. The earliest civilizations arose on the banks of perennial rivers.

💧 The Water Cycle (Hydrological Cycle)

Water moves continuously: evaporation → clouds → precipitation → run-off / infiltration → groundwater → back to ocean

🌊 Sources of Water

  • Rainfall — most important source; only 3% of total water is fresh
  • Groundwater — hidden in aquifers; only 0.003% readily available
  • Surface water — rivers, lakes, ponds, wetlands
  • Glaciers — locked in polar ice caps
Aquifer Types: Confined (sandwiched between impermeable layers) and Unconfined (with a water table).

⚠️ Effects of Over-utilization of Groundwater

  • Reduced surface water flow
  • Lowering of water table — in Gujarat, fallen by up to 40 m
  • Water logging & salinity problems
  • Ground subsidence — damage to buildings, pipes
  • Degradation of water quality (salt water intrusion)
  • Increased power costs to lift water higher

🌊 Water Calamities

Flood Unusually large amount of water at one place. Types: Flash floods, River floods, Coastal floods. Annual rainfall in India varies greatly by region → floods & droughts.
Drought Severe deficiency of water supply due to consistently below-average rainfall. Types: Meteorological, Hydrological, Agricultural. In 2005, parts of the Amazon basin experienced the worst drought in 100 years.

⚔️ Water Conflicts

Water scarcity leads to conflicts at all levels — international, national, and state. India faces inter-state disputes in Tamil Nadu–Karnataka (Kaveri), Rajasthan–Gujarat (Sardar Sarovar), Punjab–Haryana (SYL canal), etc.

📍 Case Study

Kaveri Dispute — Karnataka vs Tamil Nadu

Karnataka built three dams on the Kaveri river reducing summer flows to Tamil Nadu. The dispute dates back to 1892. In 1991, the Supreme Court appointed a Tribunal. The issue remains unresolved and has led to state-level violence ("bandhs").

🏗️ Dams — Benefits & Problems

✅ Advantages
  • Irrigation for huge agricultural projects
  • Electricity production (hydro power)
  • Navigation on otherwise unnavigable rivers
  • Recreation (boating, fishing)
  • Flood control
  • Water supply for domestic purposes
❌ Disadvantages
  • Deforestation & loss of biodiversity
  • Displacement of tribal people (40–80 million worldwide)
  • Submerges agricultural & forest land
  • Increased frequency of earthquakes
  • Water-borne diseases (malaria, schistosomiasis)
  • Siltation of reservoirs
  • Emissions of greenhouse gases from reservoirs
📍 Case Study

Sardar Sarovar Dam — Uprooted Forests and Tribal People

The Narmada dam project across Gujarat, Maharashtra, and Madhya Pradesh will submerge 573 villages. About 40,000 ha of forest under Narmada Sagar will disappear. Social activist Medha Patkar went on a 20-day hunger strike in 2006 to protest the raising of dam height. The Supreme Court refused the Narmada Bachao Andolan's appeal to stop construction.

2.4 Mineral Resources

Types of minerals and environmental effects of mining

🪨 What are Minerals?

Minerals are naturally occurring chemical compounds formed through inorganic processes under the Earth's crust. India is rich in 35 minerals including iron, aluminium, manganese, chromium, limestone, dolomite, and mica. Minerals are non-renewable — once mined, they cannot be restored or replaced.

⛏️ Environmental Effects of Mining

  • Accelerates destruction of forests & damages surface features of Earth
  • Leads to soil erosion & reduces soil fertility
  • Generates large amounts of waste, lowering land quality
  • Fumes from smelters pollute air over large areas
  • Mining causes air, water, and land pollution (poisonous gases during blasting)
  • Lowers water table; causes ground subsidence and groundwater pollution
  • Earthquakes and volcanic events can result from disturbing Earth's core
  • Occupational health hazards: asbestosis, silicosis, black lung disease
  • Combustion of coal and petroleum → radioactive pollutants, ash, trace metals

2.5 Food Resources

Types, world food problems, modern agriculture, and its effects

🌾

Agricultural Crops

Cereals, pulses, fruits, vegetables. 20 species provide most of world's food including wheat, rice, maize, potato, banana.

🐄

Livestock

Milk, meat, eggs, honey. Bird species like chickens are also food sources globally.

🐟

Fish

A major food source worldwide. Aquaculture (fresh water) and Mariculture (marine water) for artificial production.

😟 World Food Problems

  • Insufficient production — many countries face acute food shortage
  • Lack of irrigation — water scarcity impedes grain production
  • Undernutrition & malnutrition — one in four children malnourished by 2020
  • Hoarding & black marketing — creates artificial scarcity
  • Natural hazards — floods, drought, earthquakes damage agriculture
  • Poor distribution — unequal access; US food aid feeds fewer people each year
  • Population growth — food resource diminishing as population rises

🌱 Effects of Modern Agriculture on Environment

  • Soil erosion — excessive ploughing accelerates erosion by wind and water
  • Sedimentation — degraded water quality, reduced water body depth
  • Fertilizer problems — excess fertilizer → eutrophication in water bodies
  • Pesticide problems — kill non-target organisms; enter food chain
  • Water logging — excess irrigation raises water table, increases salinity
  • Climate change — agriculture adds CO₂; deforestation for cropland
  • Loss of genetic diversity — monocultures replace traditional diverse varieties
  • Intensification of inequity — poor farmers cannot afford seeds & fertilizers
🌿 Sustainable Agriculture: Ecologically sound, economically viable, socially just, and culturally appropriate. It has three goals — environmental health, economic profitability, and social and economic equity.

🐛 Fertilizers & Pesticides

Chemical Fertilizers — Adverse Effects Excess use → loss of soil fertility. Chemical fertilizers create nitrate pollution in groundwater. Run-off fertilizers speed growth of algae in water bodies (eutrophication). Nitrogen leads to accumulation of nitrates in soil.
Pesticides — Harmful Effects Not species-specific — kill non-target organisms. Adversely affect frogs, snakes, birds (natural pest controllers). Resistance builds over time. Enter food chain through biomagnification. Cause air, water, and soil pollution.

🐂 Overgrazing

Grazing by a large number of animals for extended periods without recovery time. Causes include continuously increasing livestock population.

  • Eliminates sensitive, palatable plant species → dominance of alien species
  • Accelerates soil erosion and damages soil fertility
  • Adds accumulated animal wastes to downstream water bodies (eutrophication)
  • Increases land surface reflectivity → lowers temperature of rain, increases drought chance

☀️ Solar Energy

The Sun is the ultimate source of energy on Earth. Three main applications:

  • Solar cells (PV/Photovoltaic) — convert light directly into electricity
  • Solar water heating — pipes in black-painted panels on rooftops
  • Solar furnaces — array of mirrors concentrating Sun's energy into a small space for very high temperatures
Disadvantages: Low efficiency, high initial cost, lack of adequate storage. Silicon (needed for cells) is in short supply.

💨 Wind Energy

Used for centuries for ships and windmills. Now used for supplemental electricity. Wind power is highly variable but best for individual home owners.

  • India's wind-power potential estimated at 20,000 MW
  • Currently generating ~1,020 MW
  • Largest wind farm near Kanyakumari, Tamil Nadu — generates 380 MW
Disadvantages: Takes large spaces, destroys scenic beauty, not available everywhere continuously.

🌋 Geothermal Energy

Places where surface temperature is sufficiently high to produce steam which drives turbines. Very high efficiency and low initial cost. Probably generated from decaying radioactive materials (thorium, uranium, potassium) in Earth's interior. Applications: industrial heating and electricity generation.

🌿 Biomass Energy

Organic matter that can be burnt as fuel or converted to a more convenient form. One of the most important renewable energy sources from ancient times.

  • Wood, agricultural residues, municipal solid waste
  • Sugar cane can be fermented to make alcohol (ethanol) — Brazil uses 100% ethanol in 20% of vehicles
  • Plant & animal wastes produce biofuels: methanol, natural gas, oil

♻️ Energy Recovery

Getting energy by burning waste. The calorific value of hydrocarbon plastics is comparable to that of oil. Benefits include reduced coal-fired power plants, improved air quality, slower climate change, and lower fuel bills.

Advantage of Waste-to-Energy Plants: Extract energy from trash while incinerators only reduce volume. They reduce trash volume by 90% and have much lower air pollution.

2.7 Land Resources

Soil profile, land degradation, soil erosion, desertification, and wetlands

🌍 Importance of Land

  • Human civilization has taken shape on land
  • Land is used for agriculture
  • Contains huge amount of mineral deposits
  • Contains water in underground aquifers
  • Most animals have their habitat on land
  • Provides all resources to fulfil basic human needs: food, cloth, shelter

💧 Functions of Soil

  • Significant role in nutrient cycles; releases and stores nutrients for ecosystems
  • Basis of agricultural production
  • Stores water and regulates water supplies
  • Regulates emission of trace gases
  • Degrades pollutants & filters groundwater
  • Provides foundation for buildings & structures

📉 Land Degradation

A number of natural and anthropogenic factors lower the quality of land. The UN Environment Programme estimates that 250 million people are directly affected and more than 1 billion are at risk.

🌿 Natural Factors
  • Heavy rains → removal of topsoil
  • High-speed winds → accelerate soil erosion
  • Natural disasters (earthquakes, floods)
  • Expansion of desert (overgrazing, drought)
🏭 Anthropogenic Factors
  • Mining — generates waste, destroys vegetation
  • Urbanization — encroaches on fertile land
  • Deforestation — destroys biogeochemical cycles
  • Overgrazing — exposes soil to erosion
  • Construction of dams & canals
  • Excessive use of chemical fertilizers

🌊 Soil Erosion — Causes & Effects

Soil erosion = detachment and transportation of topsoil from its resting place by wind and water. Also known as the "creeping death of land."

Causes
  • Large-scale deforestation (responsible for 30% of Earth's seriously eroded land)
  • Floods in rivers (rapid water flow along slopes)
  • Overgrazing by cattle (accounts for 35% of world's soil erosion)
  • Dry violent winds
  • Improper agricultural techniques (absence of contour building)
Effects
  • Decreased productivity of land
  • Desertification of land
  • Deposition in water bodies → clogs rivers, causes flooding
  • Loss of revenue from agricultural land
  • Land becomes unfit for agriculture

🏜️ Desertification

Conversion of fertile, non-desertic land into infertile desertic land. The UN (1992) defined it as land degradation in arid, semi-arid, and subhumid areas resulting from climatic variations and human activities.

Scale of Problem: Every year an area roughly equal to Britain is being converted to desert. 35% of Earth's land surface is at risk. 45,000,000 km² affected. 6,000,000 ha of agricultural land lost every year!
🌿 Natural Causes
  • Very low rainfall / prolonged drought
  • Excessive evaporation
  • Vast diurnal temperature extremes
  • High soil salinity
🏭 Anthropogenic Causes
  • Continuous cutting of trees
  • Heavy overgrazing
  • Over-irrigation (water logging + salinity)
  • Excessive ploughing
  • Excessive use of fertilizers

🌿 Wetlands and Their Functions

Wetlands are lands transitional between terrestrial and aquatic eco-systems, where the water table is at or near the surface, or land is covered by shallow water. Often called "kidneys of the landscape."

  • Retain water during dry periods → keep water table high
  • Mitigate flood and trap suspended solids & nutrients
  • Important feeding and breeding grounds for wildlife
  • Provide stopping place and refuge for waterfowl
  • Support species diversity
  • Recycle nutrients, purify water, attenuate floods
  • Maintain stream flow, recharge groundwater
  • India's wetlands support 2400 species and subspecies of birds
⚠️ Wetland Losses: Draining wetlands has depleted groundwater recharge — in rural India, about 6,000 villages are without a drinking water source. Introduction of exotic species like water hyacinth threatens native vegetation.

2.8 & 2.9 Conservation & Equitable Use

Individual role in conservation and sustainable lifestyle

⚡ Energy Conservation (Individual Level)

  • Save electricity → save coal (major source of world's electricity)
  • Replace incandescent bulbs with fluorescent or 100-W bulbs instead of two 60-W
  • Ride bicycles whenever possible
  • Use blank black computer screen instead of screensavers
  • Unplug electronics and kitchen appliances when not in use

🌲 Forest Conservation (Individual Level)

  • Use both sides of paper; avoid unnecessary printing
  • Reuse and recycle paper; buy recycled paper
  • Plant trees to prevent further forest degradation
  • Make equitable use of forest products

💧 Water Conservation

  • Set lawnmower blade high (2–3 inches) → fewer watering needs
  • A household can save up to 20,000 gallons/year by not leaving faucet running during teeth brushing, shaving, washing dishes
  • Wash car at home instead of self-service car wash

🥗 Sustainable Food

  • Vegetarian food habits use less water, fuel, and other natural resources
  • Eat unpackaged food — vegetables and dry beans instead of canned food
  • Eat locally available fruits and vegetables
  • Avoid food waste — plan meals carefully

🌏 Equitable Use of Resources for Sustainable Lifestyle

Equitable use means minimizing inequality within and among nations, maintaining sustainable harvest. To ensure equitable use:

  • Encourage recycling of resources and reuse paper
  • Use alternative energy sources such as solar energy for domestic heating
  • Make equitable use of water for personal and domestic purposes
  • Ensure better use and maintenance of non-renewable resources
  • Prefer usage of renewable and non-conventional energy sources
